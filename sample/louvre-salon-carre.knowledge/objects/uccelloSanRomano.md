# uccelloSanRomano

## Title

Battle of San Romano: The Counter-Attack of Micheletto Attendolo da Cotignola

## Artist

Paolo Uccello

## Identification

- date: ca. 1450-1475
- medium: tempera on wood
- dimensions: about 1.82 x 3.17 m
- Louvre location: Salle 708, Aile Denon, Niveau 1

## What Is Shown

- one panel from a three-part cycle on the Battle of San Romano of 1432
- specifically the counter-attack of Micheletto Attendolo da Cotignola
- originally part of a decorative ensemble for a Florentine palace interior

## Talking Points

- fruehe Renaissance
- beruehmt fuer Perspektivdrang und Lanzenrhythmen
- Schlacht und Geometrie stehen in eigentuemlicher Spannung
- Gewalt erscheint zugleich geordnet und kuenstlich gebaut

## What To Notice

- spears, horses and fallen weapons work almost like a formal grid
- the battle can feel dazzlingly artificial rather than purely chaotic
- Uccello turns combat into a display of design, rhythm and constructed space
- the panel's long format supports sweeping movement and staged clarity

## Why It Matters

- this is a classic case for discussing perspective not only as science, but as visual ambition
- the painting shows how Renaissance order can become nearly theatrical
- it is ideal for talking about the tension between historical violence and aesthetic control

## Comparison Hooks

- compared with Fra Angelico, Uccello is less devotional and more experimental in spatial construction
- compared with Giotto, narrative event becomes more stylized and formally orchestrated
- compared with Cimabue, frontal sacred hierarchy gives way to engineered movement across a broad field

## Provenance Notes

- part of a set of three paintings on the battle of 1432
- likely commissioned for Lionardo Bartolini Salimbeni's Florentine palace
- later associated with the Medici collections
- entered the Louvre in the nineteenth century through the Campana acquisition
